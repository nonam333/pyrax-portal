## Frequently Asked Questions

**Q: How much money do I need to start using DeFi?**

A: Technically, you can start with any amount, but practically speaking, you need enough to make gas fees worthwhile. On Ethereum mainnet, consider $1,000+ minimum. On Layer 2 solutions like Arbitrum or Polygon, you can start with as little as $100-200 since fees are much lower.

**Q: Is DeFi legal in the United States?**

A: Yes, using DeFi protocols is legal in the US. However, the regulatory landscape is evolving. The SEC has taken action against some DeFi projects, and you're responsible for paying taxes on all DeFi earnings. Some protocols may restrict US users due to regulatory uncertainty.

**Q: Can I lose more money than I put in?**

A: In most DeFi activities (lending, providing liquidity), you can only lose what you deposit. However, if you're using leverage or trading derivatives, you can lose more than your initial investment. Always understand the maximum loss potential before participating.

**Q: What happens if a DeFi protocol gets hacked while my money is in it?**

A: In most cases, your funds would be lost. Unlike traditional banks with FDIC insurance, DeFi protocols typically have no insurance or guarantees. This is why it's critical to only use well-audited protocols, never put all your capital in one place, and consider DeFi insurance for large positions.

**Q: How do I know if a DeFi protocol is safe?**

A: Check for: (1) Security audits from reputable firms like CertiK or Trail of Bits, (2) Time in operation (6+ months preferred), (3) High TVL (Total Value Locked) indicating trust from others, (4) Transparent team (not anonymous), (5) Active community and development, (6) No history of exploits or issues.

**Q: Can I withdraw my money anytime?**

A: Usually yes, but there are exceptions. Most lending protocols let you withdraw instantly (minus gas fees). Some protocols have lock-up periods or withdrawal queues. Always check the specific terms before depositing. Also remember that during extreme network congestion, high gas fees might make withdrawal temporarily impractical.

**Q: Do I need to report DeFi earnings on my taxes?**

A: Yes. In the US, all DeFi earnings are taxable. Interest earned, farming rewards claimed, and trading profits all must be reported to the IRS. Failure to report can result in penalties and interest. Use crypto tax software to track everything automatically.

**Q: What's the difference between APY and APR in DeFi?**

A: APR (Annual Percentage Rate) is simple interest without compounding. APY (Annual Percentage Yield) includes compound interest. For example, 10% APR with daily compounding becomes 10.52% APY. DeFi often shows APY since interest typically compounds automatically.

**Q: Can I use DeFi on my phone?**

A: Yes. Most DeFi protocols have mobile-compatible websites, and wallets like MetaMask, Trust Wallet, and Rainbow have mobile apps. However, complex DeFi strategies are easier to manage on desktop. Always be extra careful with mobile transactions—verify everything before confirming.

**Q: What if I send crypto to the wrong address?**

A: Blockchain transactions are irreversible. If you send to the wrong address, your funds are likely lost forever. This is why it's crucial to always send a small test transaction first, double-check addresses character by character, and use address book features in your wallet for frequently-used addresses.

## Glossary of DeFi Terms

**APY (Annual Percentage Yield)**: The yearly return on investment including compound interest.

**Automated Market Maker (AMM)**: A smart contract system that enables trading without traditional order books, using liquidity pools instead.

**Collateral**: Assets deposited to secure a loan in DeFi lending protocols.

**DEX (Decentralized Exchange)**: A platform for trading cryptocurrencies directly peer-to-peer without a centralized intermediary.

**Gas Fees**: Transaction costs on blockchain networks, paid to miners/validators for processing transactions.

**Governance Token**: A token that gives holders voting rights on protocol decisions and upgrades.

**Impermanent Loss**: The potential loss liquidity providers face when token prices diverge from their initial deposit ratio.

**Liquidity Pool**: A collection of tokens locked in a smart contract used to facilitate trading on decentralized exchanges.

**Liquidity Provider (LP)**: Someone who deposits tokens into a liquidity pool to earn trading fees and rewards.

**LP Token**: A token representing your share of a liquidity pool, which can be redeemed for your deposited assets plus earned fees.

**Liquidation**: The automatic selling of collateral when its value falls below the minimum threshold required to secure a loan.

**LTV (Loan-to-Value)**: The ratio of borrowed amount to collateral value, expressed as a percentage.

**Oracle**: A service that provides external data (like prices) to smart contracts on the blockchain.

**Protocol**: A set of smart contracts that provide specific DeFi services (lending, trading, etc.).

**Rug Pull**: A scam where developers abandon a project and steal user funds.

**Slippage**: The difference between expected trade price and actual execution price, common in low-liquidity pools.

**Smart Contract**: Self-executing code on a blockchain that automatically enforces the terms of an agreement.

**Stablecoin**: A cryptocurrency designed to maintain a stable value, typically pegged to $1 USD.

**TVL (Total Value Locked)**: The total amount of assets deposited in a DeFi protocol, indicating its size and trust level.

**Wallet**: Software or hardware that stores your private keys and allows you to interact with blockchain networks.

**Yield Farming**: The practice of moving crypto between protocols to maximize returns from interest and rewards.

## When Things Go Wrong: Emergency Procedures

### Scenario 1: You Think Your Wallet is Compromised

**Immediate Actions:**
1. STOP - Don't panic, think clearly
2. Open MetaMask/wallet on different device if possible
3. Transfer all assets to a new, secure wallet immediately
4. Prioritize most valuable assets first
5. Go to revoke.cash and revoke all token approvals
6. Consider the gas fee cost vs. asset value

**After Emergency:**
- Create new wallet with new seed phrase
- Never use compromised wallet again
- Review how compromise occurred
- Update security practices
- Enable 2FA everywhere possible

### Scenario 2: Risk of Liquidation

**Warning Signs:**
- LTV approaching 70-75%
- Collateral value dropping rapidly
- Unable to access wallet due to high gas fees

**Immediate Actions:**
1. Check exact LTV ratio on protocol dashboard
2. Calculate how much collateral needed to prevent liquidation
3. Option A: Add more collateral immediately
4. Option B: Repay part of the loan
5. Option C: If gas fees too high, accept liquidation may occur

**Prevention:**
- Never borrow more than 50% LTV
- Set up automated alerts at 60% LTV
- Keep emergency ETH for gas always available
- Consider using protocols on Layer 2 for lower fees

### Scenario 3: Protocol Gets Exploited

**If you have active positions:**
1. Check official protocol channels (Twitter, Discord) for updates
2. DO NOT panic sell or make hasty decisions
3. Wait for official guidance from protocol team
4. Check if your specific position is affected
5. Follow emergency withdrawal procedures if announced
6. Be wary of scam messages offering "help"

**After the incident:**
- Wait for post-mortem report
- Evaluate if funds are recoverable
- Consider if continuing to use protocol is safe
- Learn from what happened
- Diversify across multiple protocols going forward

### Scenario 4: Sent Funds to Wrong Address

**Unfortunately:**
- Blockchain transactions are irreversible
- Funds sent to wrong address are likely permanently lost
- No customer service can reverse this

**Only possible recovery:**
- If you sent to a centralized exchange address and can prove ownership
- If you sent to your own address on wrong network (may be recoverable with technical help)
- If recipient is known and willing to return (rare)

**Prevention is only solution:**
- Always send test transaction first
- Copy-paste addresses, never type manually
- Use address book/contact features
- Double-check destination chain
- Verify first and last characters of address
